\section{Context and Motivation}
\label{sec:intro:context}

\gls{ai} planning is a subfield of Artificial Intelligence that focuses on the automated generation of action sequences, known as \emph{plans}, to transition a system from an initial state to a desired goal state~\cite{Georgievski2023}.
As a core technology for deliberative reasoning, \gls{ai} planning enables autonomous agents to devise and execute strategies for efficiently achieving complex objectives~\cite{Chrpa2020, Guan2023}.

A defining characteristic of modern, domain-independent planning is its strict \emph{modular separation} between the planning engine (the search algorithm) and the domain model (the formal description of the environment's dynamics).
Domain models are typically specified in the \gls{pddl}~\cite{McDermott1998}, a standardised representation that declaratively describes the predicates, actions, preconditions, and effects of a planning task.
This modularity enables a single planner to operate across diverse application domains without modification, greatly increasing the reusability and versatility of planning technology~\cite{McDermott1998}.

While this separation makes planners highly general, it introduces a subtle but consequential challenge: \emph{structural sensitivity}.
Planning engines employ vastly different internal algorithms to parse \gls{pddl} files, construct intermediate representations, and search for valid plans.
\citeauthor{Vallati2015}~\cite{Vallati2015, Vallati2021} have demonstrated that the purely textual arrangement of a domain model---the ordering of actions, predicates, preconditions, and effects---can cause orders-of-magnitude differences in planner runtime, coverage, and even competition rankings, \emph{without any change to the domain's semantics}.
Earlier studies by \citeauthor{HoweDahlman2002}~\cite{HoweDahlman2002} and \citeauthor{RiddleHolteBarley2011}~\cite{RiddleHolteBarley2011} corroborate this finding, showing that a structural configuration beneficial for one planner may simultaneously degrade the performance of another.

Currently, optimising the textual structure of domain models is a manual, trial-and-error process that demands expert intuition and deep knowledge of planner internals~\cite{McCluskey2017}.
Automated configuration tools are needed not only to reduce this labour-intensive effort, but also to tailor domain models specifically for the planner that will ultimately process them, thereby achieving faster planning times, higher solution quality, and more energy-efficient computation.

\section{State of the Art and Research Gap}
\label{sec:intro:gap}

The sensitivity of planners to domain model structure is well established in the planning literature.
\citeauthor{Vallati2015}~\cite{Vallati2015} introduced the first systematic approach to domain model configuration by formulating it as an algorithm configuration problem and employing a sequential model-based method to optimise structural orderings.
In an extended study, \citeauthor{Vallati2021}~\cite{Vallati2021} deepened this investigation by expanding the set of configurable elements and introducing strategic placement of learned macro-operators.
While these approaches yield high-quality configurations, they depend on extensive, offline planner evaluations---requiring numerous costly runs---and operate within a relatively narrow configuration space.

Recent advances in \glspl{llm} have opened a fundamentally new paradigm for domain model configuration.
\glspl{llm} are probabilistic next-token predictors trained on large text corpora that have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating, editing, and refactoring code-like artefacts from natural-language instructions~\cite{Zhao2023, Minaee2024}.
Several studies have explored their use for generating \gls{pddl} domain models from scratch~\cite{Oswald2024, Guan2023, Smirnov2024, Casciani2025, Huang2025}, while broader analyses emphasise that \glspl{llm} can support knowledge engineering in automated planning but require external validators to ensure correctness~\cite{Vallati2025}.

The most directly relevant prior work is the bachelor thesis of \citeauthor{Elis2025}~\cite{Elis2025} and the associated study by \citeauthor{Georgievski2025}~\cite{Georgievski2025}, which investigated whether \glspl{llm} can serve as domain model configurators through single-forward-pass generation.
Their approach applied a set of domain-engineering reordering principles via various prompting strategies (zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought) and evaluated the resulting configurations across seven \glspl{llm}, five planners, and five benchmark domains.
Their findings established three key results: (i)~\glspl{llm} can produce syntactically valid and semantically equivalent domain configurations in a substantial proportion of cases; (ii)~the choice of \gls{llm} dominates prompting strategy and temperature as the primary determinant of configuration quality; and (iii)~performance improvements are highly planner-dependent, with some planners consistently benefiting while others consistently degrading.

Despite these advances, two critical research gaps remain:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Lack of architecture awareness.}
        Existing \gls{llm}-based configurators employ \emph{planner-agnostic} prompts: they apply generic reordering principles without any knowledge of the target planner's internal architecture.
        Because distinct planner architectures---such as heuristic forward search, width-based search, decoupled search, and compilation to \gls{sat}---process and evaluate domains through fundamentally different algorithmic pipelines, generic structural optimisation yields inconsistent results.
        An \gls{llm}-generated reordering that improves one planner may degrade another~\cite{Georgievski2025}.

  \item \textbf{Absence of execution-based feedback.}
        All prior \gls{llm}-based approaches generate domain configurations in a single forward pass, without access to quantitative feedback from actual planner executions.
        This precludes iterative refinement: the \gls{llm} cannot learn from the consequences of its structural choices or correct configuration errors that only manifest at runtime.
        While the \emph{LLM-Modulo} framework proposed by \citeauthor{Kambhampati2024}~\cite{Kambhampati2024} advocates combining \glspl{llm} with external verifiers and feedback loops, no prior work has instantiated this paradigm for domain model configuration.
\end{enumerate}

These gaps motivate the central hypothesis of the present thesis: because distinct planner architectures process and evaluate domain models through different algorithmic pipelines, \emph{architecture-aware} prompting combined with iterative execution feedback can enable \glspl{llm} to produce domain configurations that are specifically optimised for a given target planner.

\section{Research Questions}
\label{sec:intro:research_questions}

Building on the identified research gaps, this thesis investigates the following main research question:

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Main Research Question (MRQ):}
  \emph{To what extent can \glspl{llm} dynamically optimise the structural configuration of \gls{pddl} domain models for distinct underlying planner architectures, using targeted prompting, iterative execution feedback, and comparative analysis?}
\end{quote}

To decompose this overarching question into tractable components, we formulate the following sub-questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=1.5cm, labelwidth=1cm]
  \item[\textbf{SQ1:}]
    What are the architecture models of state-of-the-art planners, which components (e.g., parsing, translation, grounding, search, heuristics) are present across different planner architecture models, and how are those components implemented?

  \item[\textbf{SQ2:}]
    Can \glspl{llm} successfully apply targeted structural transformations when explicitly prompted with the underlying mechanics of a specific planner architecture?

  \item[\textbf{SQ3:}]
    To what degree does an LLM-Modulo feedback loop---providing the \gls{llm} with quantitative execution metrics---improve the efficiency of domain configurations compared to single-pass generations?

  \item[\textbf{SQ4:}]
    How effectively can an automated validation pipeline prevent the \gls{llm} from hallucinating semantic changes during multi-turn iterative refinement?

  \item[\textbf{SQ5:}]
    How do different planner architectures respond to structural variations in \gls{pddl} domain models, and what empirical patterns can be derived to map specific textual configurations to their ideal target architectures?
\end{description}

\section{Methodology and Contributions}
\label{sec:intro:methodology}

We employ a quantitative, experimental, and analytical methodology structured around a multi-stage experimental pipeline comprising four configuration stages:

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=2.5cm, labelwidth=2.2cm]
  \item[\textbf{Stage~0}] \textbf{(Baseline):}
    Original, unmodified \gls{pddl} domains are executed against all selected planners to establish baseline performance measurements.

  \item[\textbf{Stage~1}] \textbf{(General Prompt):}
    Each \gls{llm} receives a planner-agnostic prompt requesting general structural optimisation of the domain, serving as the control condition against which architecture-aware interventions are measured.

  \item[\textbf{Stage~2}] \textbf{(Architecture-Aware Prompt):}
    Each \gls{llm} receives a prompt that explicitly encodes the solver type and provides synthesised heuristic rules specific to the target planner's architecture, derived from a systematic component-level analysis of each planner's internal architecture (see \Cref{ch:design}).
    This stage addresses \textbf{SQ2}.

  \item[\textbf{Stage~3}] \textbf{(LLM-Modulo Feedback Loop):}
    A programmatic meta-controller iteratively feeds quantitative execution telemetry (e.g., states expanded, search time) back to the \gls{llm} as diagnostic natural-language feedback, enabling multi-turn refinement of the domain configuration.
    This stage addresses \textbf{SQ3}.
\end{description}

A four-level validation pipeline ensures semantic equivalence at every stage by performing \gls{pddl} extraction, syntactic checking via the official VAL tool, identity checking, and deep semantic equivalence verification (\textbf{SQ4}).
The final configurations are formally benchmarked against the full suite of problem instances from the \gls{ipc} Agile Tracks.
A four-stage ablation study isolates the incremental value of each stage, and statistical significance is validated using non-parametric tests.

The evaluation spans four architecturally diverse planners (\gls{bfws}, \gls{lama}, DecStar, and Madagascar), five \gls{ipc} benchmark domains, and four state-of-the-art \glspl{llm}.

The original contributions of this thesis are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Architecture-aware domain model configuration.}
        We introduce the first approach that explicitly conditions \gls{llm}-based domain configuration on the internal architecture of the target planner, moving beyond planner-agnostic strategies.

  \item \textbf{Iterative LLM-Modulo feedback loop for domain configuration.}
        We instantiate the LLM-Modulo paradigm proposed by \citeauthor{Kambhampati2024}~\cite{Kambhampati2024} for domain model configuration, demonstrating that execution-based feedback substantially outperforms single-pass generation.

  \item \textbf{Four-level automated validation pipeline.}
        We design and implement a validation pipeline that prevents semantic hallucinations during multi-turn iterative refinement, extending the three-stage pipeline of \citeauthor{Georgievski2025}~\cite{Georgievski2025} with an additional extraction stage.

  \item \textbf{Cross-architecture empirical analysis.}
        We provide a comprehensive four-stage ablation study that isolates the contribution of architecture-aware prompting and the feedback loop, yielding actionable empirical patterns that map structural configurations to target architectures (\textbf{SQ5}).

  \item \textbf{Open-source, reproducible experimental artefacts.}
        All code, prompts, domain configurations, raw execution logs, and analysis scripts are published under the MIT licence to enable full reproducibility.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Structure of the Thesis}
\label{sec:intro:structure}

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows:

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=2.5cm, labelwidth=2.2cm]
  \item[\Cref{ch:background}] introduces the background concepts essential for understanding the thesis, including automated planning and \gls{pddl}, search paradigms in classical planning, domain model configuration, \glspl{llm} and prompting strategies, and the LLM-Modulo framework.

  \item[\Cref{ch:related_work}] surveys related work across four areas: classical domain model configuration, \gls{llm}-based domain model configuration, \glspl{llm} as domain modellers, and \glspl{llm} as planners. The chapter concludes by explicitly positioning the present thesis within the identified research gap.

  \item[\Cref{ch:design}] presents the abstract design of the experimental pipeline, detailing the four configuration stages (Stages~0--3), the four-level validation pipeline, and the computational safeguards.

  \item[\Cref{ch:implementation}] provides the implementation details of the pipeline, including planner integration, prompt design, \gls{llm} interface, validation modules, and the feedback loop controller.

  \item[\Cref{ch:experimental_setup}] describes the experimental setup, specifying the selected planners, domains, \glspl{llm}, hyperparameters, and evaluation metrics.

  \item[\Cref{ch:results}] reports and discusses the experimental results, organised by stage-specific analysis and cross-stage comparative analysis.

  \item[\Cref{ch:threats}] identifies threats to the validity of the study, covering internal, external, construct, and conclusion validity.

  \item[\Cref{ch:conclusion}] concludes the thesis by summarising the main findings, providing definitive answers to the research questions, discussing limitations, and outlining directions for future work.
\end{description}
