\chapter{Results}\label{ch:results}
% Pure results presentation and interpretation — no design or methodology

This chapter presents the empirical results of the four-stage configuration pipeline designed in \Cref{ch:design} and parameterised in \Cref{ch:experimental_setup}.
The presentation is deliberately evaluative rather than methodological: every stage is reported against the same two primary outcome measures.
The first is \emph{coverage}---the number of benchmark instances a planner solves within the 360-second limit.
The second is the \emph{\gls{ipc} score} of \Cref{eq:ipc_score}, a normalised per-instance efficiency measure that rewards fast solutions, ranges from~0 (unsolved) to~1 (matching the best observed time), and is computed against a single, globally consistent reference time once the complete experiment has finished.
Because both measures are evaluated per instance and only then aggregated, every comparison reported here is made on identical (domain, instance, planner) units rather than across heterogeneous subsets---a property whose importance becomes apparent already in the baseline.

The chapter follows the logical progression of the pipeline.
\Cref{sec:results:stage0} characterises the unmodified baseline (Stage~0), establishing the reference point against which every later configuration is scored.
\Cref{sec:results:stage1,sec:results:stage2,sec:results:stage3} then report the three configuration stages in turn---the general prompt, the architecture-aware prompt, and the feedback loop.
\Cref{sec:results:cross_stage} synthesises the full progression, \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation} provides the confirmatory statistical analysis, and \Cref{sec:results:prior_work} positions the findings relative to the prior work of \citeauthor{Elis2025}~\cite{Elis2025} and \citeauthor{Georgievski2025}~\cite{Georgievski2025}.

\section{Baseline Characterisation (Stage 0)}
\label{sec:results:stage0}

Stage~0 executes the four target planners on the five original, unmodified \gls{ipc} domains, with no \gls{llm} in the loop.
As set out in \Cref{sec:design:stage0}, its purpose is twofold.
First, it records the \emph{reference} runtimes $T_i$ against which all later \gls{ipc} score gains are computed (\Cref{eq:ipc_score}).
Second, it characterises the difficulty landscape---which domains are intrinsically hard, which planners struggle, and, most importantly, \emph{where measurable headroom for configuration actually exists}.
The full factorial design comprises $4~\text{planners} \times 5~\text{domains} \times 15~\text{instances} = 300$ runs.

\subsection{Aggregate Execution Outcome}
\label{sec:results:stage0:aggregate}

The complete baseline sweep finished in 5~hours 14~minutes of wall-clock pipeline time.
Of the 300 runs, 168 (56.0\%) terminated in \texttt{SUCCESS} and the remaining 132 (44.0\%) in \texttt{TIMEOUT}.
Critically, \emph{no run produced a \texttt{MEMOUT} or \texttt{FAILURE}}: under the 8~GB memory cap, every planner either solved an instance within the time limit or exhausted that limit while still searching.
This clean, binary outcome is methodologically reassuring---it confirms that the resource envelope (\Cref{sec:setup:hyperparameters}) was correctly calibrated and that the 132 timeouts reflect genuine search intractability rather than crashes, parser rejections, or out-of-memory faults that would otherwise contaminate the reference baseline.

\Cref{tab:s0_planner_summary} summarises performance by planner.
Across the 168 successful runs, the mean wall-clock time was 48.88~s but the median only 8.47~s, with individual runs ranging from 0.41~s to 351.23~s.
This more-than-fivefold gap between mean and median exposes the heavy right-skew that characterises planning runtimes: a small minority of hard-but-solved instances dominates the arithmetic mean.
The skew is not a nuisance to be smoothed away---it is the empirical reason why every significance test in this chapter relies on the non-parametric, paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test rather than a mean-based parametric alternative (\Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}).

\subsection{Planner-Level Performance}
\label{sec:results:stage0:planners}

The four planners separate into two clear tiers.
\gls{bfws} solves 85.33\% of instances (64/75) and \gls{lama} 70.67\% (53/75), whereas DecStar and Madagascar solve only 34.67\% (26/75) and 33.33\% (25/75) respectively---less than half the coverage of the leading pair.
\gls{bfws}'s lead is consistent with its width-based search paradigm (\Cref{sec:setup:planners}).
Rather than relying on a single goal-distance heuristic, which may be poorly informed on unfamiliar domains, \gls{bfws} prioritises states that exhibit structurally \emph{novel} combinations of variable assignments; as the \gls{ipc}~2018 Agile-track winner, it is explicitly tuned to find \emph{any} valid plan quickly, an objective that aligns directly with this study's time-limited setting.
Madagascar, the only \gls{sat}-based planner, occupies the opposite extreme.
It translates each planning task into a sequence of Boolean formulae---one per candidate plan length---and delegates them to a \gls{sat} solver.
When a short plan exists this is extraordinarily fast (Madagascar is the quickest planner on Depots, mean 0.58~s), but when the optimal horizon is long or unknown the solver must iterate over ever-larger formulae, which explains why Madagascar fails outright on the long-horizon VisitAll and Ricochet Robots domains.

\paragraph{The survivorship caveat.}
A naive reading of \Cref{tab:s0_planner_summary} invites two misinterpretations that must be dispelled before any cross-planner claim is made.
First, \gls{bfws} records the \emph{highest} mean wall-clock time (66.98~s) despite being the strongest planner; second, DecStar and Madagascar record the \emph{lowest} mean plan costs (118.7 and 97.3) despite being the weakest.
Both artefacts arise from a single source---a survivorship effect.
Wall-clock time, plan cost, and states expanded are recorded only for \texttt{SUCCESS} runs, so each planner's averages are taken over a \emph{different} subset of instances.
\gls{bfws} and \gls{lama} succeed on the long-horizon VisitAll instances (whose plans run to several thousand actions; see \Cref{sec:results:stage0:cost}), and those instances inflate both their mean runtimes and their mean costs.
The weaker planners simply time out on the very same instances, so their averages are computed only over the short, easy instances they manage to solve.
Consequently, \emph{coverage is the only planner-level aggregate in \Cref{tab:s0_planner_summary} that is comparable across planners}; every efficiency metric must be compared within a fixed (domain, instance) cell.
This is the concrete empirical justification for the per-instance \gls{ipc}-score pairing used throughout the rest of this chapter.

When a like-for-like comparison \emph{is} made, \gls{bfws}'s efficiency advantage is stark.
On Ricochet Robots, for instance, \gls{bfws} expands a mean of \num{95825} states against \gls{lama}'s \num{1380370} and DecStar's \num{530587}---an order-of-magnitude economy on identical instances, confirming that its novelty heuristic prunes the search space far more aggressively than delete-relaxation heuristics or decoupled search.

\subsection{Domain Difficulty Landscape}
\label{sec:results:stage0:domains}

Aggregating over planners reveals an equally pronounced spread across domains, shown in \Cref{tab:s0_domain_difficulty}.
Depots is almost trivially easy (98.3\% solved; 59 of 60 runs), whereas Snake is the hardest (31.7\%), with Ricochet Robots close behind (36.7\%).
The ordering is structurally intuitive.
Depots (IPC~2002) is a well-studied logistics domain with a strongly typed three-level hierarchy and spatially independent sub-problems, properties that every architecture exploits effectively.
Snake (IPC~2018), by contrast, is untyped, relies on negative preconditions, and is dense with irreversible dead-end states: a single wrong move can render the goal unreachable, forcing planners to waste effort exploring doomed regions of the state space.
Ricochet Robots (IPC~2023) is hard for a different reason---its sliding-block mechanic produces long action sequences and demands that pieces be coordinated as mutual blockers, a structure that defeats most goal-distance heuristics.
The 67-percentage-point gap between the easiest and hardest domain confirms that the five-domain selection of \Cref{sec:setup:domains} spans the intended breadth of structural difficulty.

\subsection{The Planner--Domain Coverage Interaction}
\label{sec:results:stage0:coverage}

Planner-level and domain-level aggregates each tell only half the story; the decisive structure is their \emph{interaction}, visualised in the coverage heatmap of \Cref{fig:s0_coverage_heatmap} and quantified as runtimes in \Cref{tab:s0_walltime}.
Three features of this matrix shape the entire remainder of the study.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{results/s0_coverage_heatmap.png}
  \caption{Stage~0 coverage matrix: instances solved (out of 15) for each (domain, planner) pair. \gls{bfws} (leftmost column) attains the highest or joint-highest coverage in every one of the five domains. The three pale cells mark architecturally incompatible pairs that never solve a single instance, while the saturated Depots row shows a domain that every planner finds near-trivial.}
  \label{fig:s0_coverage_heatmap}
\end{figure}

First, \gls{bfws} attains the highest or joint-highest coverage in \emph{every} domain---15/15 on VisitAll, Depots, and Barman, and the single best result on the two hardest domains (8/15 on Snake, 11/15 on Ricochet Robots).
No other planner is so uniformly robust, which makes \gls{bfws} the natural reference architecture for the coverage dimension.

Second, three (domain, planner) cells are \emph{completely unsolvable}: VisitAll$\times$DecStar, VisitAll$\times$Madagascar, and Ricochet Robots$\times$Madagascar each score 0/15.
These zeros are not noise but architectural incompatibilities.
VisitAll's single agent and fully connected grid offer no independent sub-systems for DecStar's star-topology decomposition to exploit, so its factoring overhead yields only cost; and both unsolved domains require the long action horizons that are pathological for Madagascar's length-indexed \gls{sat} encoding.
These three pairs reappear in Stage~3 as the twelve always-timeout triples (three pairs across the four \glspl{llm}) that are excluded from the contestable-improvement analysis, because no purely structural reordering can be expected to bridge a fundamental paradigm mismatch.

Third, seven cells are \emph{saturated} at 15/15, but they are not equally interesting as optimisation targets.
\Cref{tab:s0_walltime} resolves them by runtime.
Four are already \emph{trivially fast}---Depots under Madagascar (0.58~s), \gls{lama} (1.99~s), and \gls{bfws} (3.13~s), and Barman under \gls{lama} (3.62~s)---leaving essentially no runtime headroom, so that any Stage~1--3 gain on these cells can come only from plan-quality improvements.
The other three saturated cells (VisitAll$\times$\gls{bfws} at 17.6~s, VisitAll$\times$\gls{lama} at 44.0~s, and Barman$\times$\gls{bfws} at 53.2~s) solve everything but slowly, and therefore offer substantial runtime that a better-structured domain could potentially recover.

Taken together, these features partition the configuration problem into three regimes that frame the later stages: \emph{saturated-fast} cells with little runtime headroom, \emph{zero-coverage} cells that are likely beyond the reach of reordering alone, and a broad \emph{contested middle}---the partially solved cells of Snake, Ricochet Robots, and the weaker planners on Barman and VisitAll---where coverage and runtime are genuinely movable and the bulk of the achievable improvement must lie.

\subsection{Plan Cost, Runtime Variance, and Measurement Scope}
\label{sec:results:stage0:cost}

The per-run trade-off between runtime and plan cost, plotted in \Cref{fig:s0_runtime_cost}, both substantiates the survivorship caveat of \Cref{sec:results:stage0:planners} and surfaces two further properties that govern how the later stages must be measured.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{results/s0_runtime_cost_scatter.png}
  \caption{Per-run wall-clock time (log scale) versus plan cost for all 168 successful baseline runs, coloured by planner and shaped by domain. The isolated upper cluster of high-cost plans belongs exclusively to \gls{bfws} and \gls{lama} on VisitAll; because DecStar and Madagascar never solve that domain, their absence from this region---not any intrinsic plan-quality advantage---explains their deceptively low mean plan costs.}
  \label{fig:s0_runtime_cost}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Plan cost is comparable only within a domain.}
The scatter makes the cost confound visually unmistakable: the isolated cluster of plans costing 2{,}800--5{,}300 actions is composed entirely of \gls{bfws} and \gls{lama} runs on VisitAll, a domain whose graph-reachability goal demands very long plans.
DecStar and Madagascar never appear in that cluster simply because they never solve VisitAll.
When cost is instead compared \emph{within} a shared domain, the apparent quality gap vanishes: on Depots all four planners produce plans of 48.7--61.8 actions, and on Barman of 197--293 actions.
No planner is therefore intrinsically cheaper; the planner-level cost averages of \Cref{tab:s0_planner_summary} are an artefact of coverage, not of solution quality.

\paragraph{Runtime variance is large even within a single cell.}
Beyond the cross-instance skew already noted, runtime varies enormously \emph{between instances of the same} (domain, planner) \emph{cell}: the coefficient of variation ranges from 0.31 (Depots$\times$Madagascar, a tightly predictable \gls{sat} workload) up to 2.57.
The extreme case is Depots$\times$DecStar, where the easiest instance solves in 0.86~s and the hardest in 53.04~s---a 62-fold spread driven purely by per-instance structure.
A naive comparison of \emph{average} solve times between the baseline and a configured domain would be swamped by this instance-level variance.
This is precisely why improvement is assessed with a paired test that compares each instance against its own baseline counterpart, isolating the effect of the configuration from the effect of instance difficulty.

\paragraph{Measurable metrics are dictated by architecture.}
Finally, the planners do not even expose the same telemetry, as \Cref{tab:s0_metric_availability} documents.
The \gls{sat}-based Madagascar reports none of the state-space metrics, because it explores no explicit state space at all; the \gls{bfws} (LAPKT) framework merges state evaluation with expansion and omits peak-memory reporting.
Only two metrics---plan cost and wall-clock runtime---are reported by all four architectures.
This is the final piece of the methodological rationale for the \gls{ipc} score: derived solely from wall-clock runtime and the binary solved/unsolved outcome, it is the one efficiency measure that can be computed uniformly for every planner, and is thus the only sound basis for the cross-architecture comparisons that follow.

\subsection{Synthesis: The Reference Landscape for Configuration}
\label{sec:results:stage0:synthesis}

The baseline delivers exactly what the later stages require.
It fixes the reference runtimes $T_i$ of \Cref{tab:s0_walltime}, and hence the per-instance baseline \gls{ipc} scores that form the zero point for every gain measured in Stages~1--3 (\Cref{eq:ipc_gain}).
It delimits the contestable space, separating the twelve always-timeout triples and the four trivially fast cells---where little or no runtime can be recovered---from the broad contested middle where improvement is genuinely possible.
And it exposes the planner-specific behavioural signatures that the architecture-aware prompts of \Cref{sec:setup:arch_aware_prompt} are explicitly designed to exploit: \gls{bfws}'s novelty-driven state economy, Madagascar's affinity for short-horizon \gls{sat} encodings, DecStar's dependence on decomposable sub-systems, and \gls{lama}'s balanced heuristic search.
With this reference landscape established, \Cref{sec:results:stage1} turns to the first intervention---the general-purpose \gls{llm} prompt of Stage~1.

\section{General Prompt Results (Stage 1)}
\label{sec:results:stage1}

Stage~1 introduces the first \gls{llm} intervention: the generic, planner-agnostic prompt of \Cref{sec:setup:general_prompt} (the $\Pi_\text{ZS-S}$ zero-shot-short strategy), which simply asks each model to reorder a domain ``to improve AI planner efficiency'' without naming or describing any target planner.
Each of the four \glspl{llm} processed all five domains, producing 20 candidate domains; those that passed validation were executed across all four planners and 15 instances, for 1{,}080 planner runs.
Because the prompt withholds all architectural knowledge, this stage functions as a controlled reference point for the configuration idea itself, and its guiding question is deliberately binary: \emph{does generic reordering, on its own, move planner performance?}

\subsection{Generation and Validation}
\label{sec:results:stage1:generation}

All 20 \gls{llm} API calls returned a complete response without error, and every response passed extraction (V1) and VAL syntax checking (V2) of the four-level validation pipeline (\Cref{sec:design:validation}).
Two outputs were rejected at the identity check (V3)---both from DeepSeek-R1, on Ricochet Robots and Barman---leaving 18 of 20 candidates (90\%) valid, all of which passed the semantic-equivalence check (V4) intact (\Cref{tab:s1_generation}).
The perfect syntax and semantic rates are themselves a finding: when instructed to emit only valid PDDL and to preserve meaning, no model introduced a syntax error or an illegal logical change. The models reliably separate reorderable surface form from load-bearing semantics.

The two rejections expose a behavioural axis among the models. DeepSeek-R1, whose reasoning architecture deliberates before answering, judged two domains already well-ordered and returned them verbatim; the identity guard (V3) rejects such outputs because they are indistinguishable from the Stage~0 baseline. The same conservatism is visible in the reordering effort of \Cref{tab:s1_generation}: DeepSeek-R1 made only 9 individual reorderings, against 28--33 for the other three models, with Claude Opus~4.6 the most active. This caution carries no efficiency benefit---DeepSeek-R1 emitted \num{10201} output tokens, roughly three times its peers, as its chain-of-thought trace inflates the completion---making it simultaneously the most expensive and the least productive model at this stage.

\subsection{What the Models Chose to Reorder}
\label{sec:results:stage1:reordering}

\Cref{fig:s1_reordering} shows how the 18 valid reorderings distribute across the nine PDDL component types.
The edits concentrate sharply on \emph{operational} components---preconditions (modified in 16 of 18 domains), actions (12), add-effects (11), delete-effects (6), and predicates (4)---while four components are \emph{never} touched: requirements, types, functions, and parameters.
Both ends of this distribution are informative.
The leading order---preconditions before actions before effects---matches the intuition of the planning literature: precondition order governs early applicability pruning during successor generation, and action order governs the sequence in which operators are first considered.
The four zeros are arguably the stronger result.
Requirements, types, and functions are declarative sections parsed once at initialisation, with no bearing on search; and parameter order is semantically load-bearing, since permuting an action's parameters would invalidate every problem file that references it.
That all four models avoided these components---parameters above all---corroborates the V4 outcome from a second direction: the reorderings are safe and operationally targeted, not random shuffling.
Absent planner-specific guidance, the models converge on the same generic intuition about what matters; they differ only in degree, not in kind.

\subsection{Planner Impact: A Controlled Null Result}
\label{sec:results:stage1:impact}

Valid and sensible as these edits are, they leave planner performance essentially unchanged.
The Stage~1 overall solve rate is 56.57\% (611/1{,}080), against the Stage~0 baseline of 56.00\%---a difference of barely half a percentage point.
\Cref{tab:s1_solve_rate} confirms that the stability holds planner by planner: no planner moves by more than 3.5 percentage points, the largest shifts being Madagascar's $+3.34$ and \gls{bfws}'s $-2.00$.
The timeout landscape is likewise frozen in place: the hardest domains (Snake, Ricochet Robots) and the most timeout-prone planners (DecStar, Madagascar) remain exactly those identified in \Cref{sec:results:stage0}, each within about two percentage points of its baseline rate.
Coverage is even invariant \emph{across} the four models---every (domain, planner) cell, from the saturated Depots\,$\times$\,\gls{bfws} (15/15) to the incompatible VisitAll\,$\times$\,DecStar (0/15), records the same coverage no matter which \gls{llm} produced the domain.

This near-perfect stability is, in one sense, exactly what a semantics-preserving transformation should produce: an instance that is fundamentally unsolvable or already trivial at baseline remains so after its components are merely reshuffled.
But the same stability is the central negative result of the stage---generic reordering does not shift the difficulty landscape.
Coverage is admittedly a coarse lens, and small per-instance runtime changes can still occur within an unchanged coverage envelope; those finer \gls{ipc}-score effects are quantified in the cross-stage analysis (\Cref{sec:results:cross_stage}).
At the level of what a planner-agnostic prompt can \emph{reliably} achieve, however, the conclusion is unambiguous: instructing a model that a domain should be ``more efficient'' is not the same as telling it how a particular planner will read that domain.
Closing precisely this gap---by injecting planner-architecture knowledge into the prompt---is the task of Stage~2 (\Cref{sec:results:stage2}).

\section{Architecture-Aware Prompt Results (Stage 2)}
\label{sec:results:stage2}

Stage~2 replaces the planner-agnostic prompt of Stage~1 with the four planner-tailored, architecture-aware prompts of \Cref{sec:setup:arch_aware_prompt}, each of which injects a synthesised description of one target planner's internal pipeline together with planner-specific reordering rules.
Every \gls{llm} now produces a separate configuration for each planner, so the design expands to $4~\text{\glspl{llm}} \times 5~\text{domains} \times 4~\text{planner prompts} = 80$ generations.
The stage answers two questions in sequence.
The first is one of \emph{efficacy}: does naming the target planner and describing how it reads a domain yield measurable improvements where the generic prompt did not?
The second, and the one that bears most directly on the thesis hypothesis, is one of \emph{specialisation}: are those improvements genuinely tied to the planner they were designed for, or do they merely make the domain better for everyone?
A dedicated cross-testing phase (\Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}) is what makes the second question answerable.

\subsection{Generation and Validation}
\label{sec:results:stage2:generation}

Of the 80 generations, 75 (93.8\%) passed validation. The five losses split into three Gemini~3.1~Pro outputs that exceeded the completion-token limit on the longest prompts (the DecStar and \gls{lama} prompts for Depots and Barman) and two GPT-5.4 outputs rejected at the semantic check~(V4)---on the \gls{lama} prompt for Ricochet Robots and the \gls{bfws} prompt for Depots---where the model went beyond reordering and altered a precondition or effect in its attempt to optimise. Claude Opus~4.6 again validated on all 20 calls.

More revealing than the pass rate is \emph{what} changed. Under the architecture-aware prompts the reordering of predicate declarations jumps to 75 of 75 valid domains (100\%), against just 22\% under the generic prompt of Stage~1 (\Cref{fig:s1_reordering}). This is the clearest behavioural fingerprint that the architectural guidance was actually absorbed: all four prompts name predicate ordering as the highest-priority lever (for mutex discovery, novelty-table indexing, or SAT variable assignment), and the models obeyed uniformly. Add-effects (87\%), preconditions (85\%), and actions (75\%) follow, while parameters (1\%) and the declarative sections (0\%) remain untouched---the same safe, operational-only discipline observed in Stage~1. As in earlier stages, DeepSeek-R1's reasoning trace inflated its output to roughly \num{106185} tokens (about seven times GPT-5.4 and Claude), with no corresponding advantage in outcome.

\subsection{Improvement Efficacy}
\label{sec:results:stage2:efficacy}

Each of the 75 validated configurations was executed on its target planner (1{,}125 runs) and evaluated by the three-condition improvement test of \Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}, which requires a statistically significant, practically positive, and coverage-preserving gain in per-instance \gls{ipc} score over the Stage~0 baseline.
\emph{Forty-two of the 75 configurations (56.0\%) qualified as improved}---a decisive departure from the inert generic reorderings of Stage~1.
Because this gate is the pipeline's internal screening step, it applies the relaxed $\alpha = 0.25$ threshold motivated in \Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}; the confirmatory analysis at the conventional $\alpha = 0.05$ is reported separately in \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation}.

The single most important result of the stage is \emph{how} that improvement rate distributes across the four target planners (\Cref{tab:s2_improvement_planner}).
The ordering---\gls{lama} (100\%) $>$ \gls{bfws} (57.9\%) $>$ DecStar (38.9\%) $>$ Madagascar (30.0\%)---tracks almost exactly how sensitive each architecture is to the textual ordering of a domain.
\gls{lama}'s entire forward-search pipeline is order-driven: predicate order shapes mutex and landmark discovery, and precondition order determines which supports the FF heuristic selects as preferred operators, so every one of its 18 configurations improved.
Madagascar sits at the opposite pole: it compiles the task to \gls{sat}, and the solver's dynamic variable-ordering heuristic (VSIDS) rapidly overrides whatever initial variable order the PDDL layout imposes, washing out most of the \gls{llm}'s structural intent.
\gls{bfws} and DecStar fall in between, consistent with their partial reliance on textual structure.
This monotonic relationship between architectural order-sensitivity and responsiveness to configuration is, in miniature, the empirical core of the thesis.

The gains themselves are real but moderate. \Cref{fig:s2_ipc_gains} shows the distribution of mean \gls{ipc} gains across all 75 configurations: a dense cluster of small positive values, a positive skew, and a ceiling near $0.09$---no single configuration exceeds a gain of $0.1$. Architecture-aware reordering therefore delivers consistent fine-tuning rather than dramatic breakthroughs, a reading reinforced by the reliability of the gains: 19 of the 42 improvements reach $p \leq 0.001$. Crucially, the improvements are almost purely about speed, not coverage: 41 of the 42 preserve baseline coverage exactly ($\Delta\text{Coverage} = 0$), the lone exception being Snake\,$\times$\,Madagascar under Claude, which solves one additional instance. The reorderings make planners solve the \emph{same} instances faster.

Depots improves in all 14 of its configurations: its high baseline coverage gives the Wilcoxon test maximal power (15 paired, non-zero instances), and its rich logistics structure offers many legal reordering opportunities. Ricochet Robots is the most resistant (26.7\%) for the opposite reasons---low baseline coverage and a deeply combinatorial search that reordering cannot reshape. Among the models, Claude Opus~4.6 leads at 65.0\%, a margin attributable less to deeper planning insight than to reliability: its perfect validation record meant all 20 of its configurations reached the planners, whereas token-limit and semantic losses thinned the fields of Gemini and GPT-5.4. The 33 configurations that did not improve are dominated by the structural floor identified in Stage~0: 12 of them are zero-coverage triples---the VisitAll\,$\times$\,\{DecStar, Madagascar\} and Ricochet\,Robots\,$\times$\,Madagascar pairs---on which both baseline and configured domains time out on every instance. Reordering cannot bridge a fundamental planner--domain incompatibility; it can only accelerate searches that already terminate.

\subsection{Architecture Specialisation}
\label{sec:results:stage2:specialisation}

Establishing that the prompts \emph{improve} the target planner is only half the claim; the thesis asserts that the improvements are \emph{specific} to that planner. To test this, every one of the 42 improved domains was re-executed on the three planners it was \emph{not} designed for---1{,}890 additional cross-test runs. For each configuration we define its \emph{specialisation index} as the target-planner \gls{ipc} gain minus the mean gain across the three non-target planners, computed on a per-instance local reference so that target and non-target cells are numerically comparable. A positive index means the optimisation favoured its intended planner. Each configuration is then classed as \emph{specialised} (non-targets are actively hurt, by more than a $0.005$ noise margin), \emph{universally better} (non-targets also gain, but no more than the target), \emph{anti-specialised} (non-targets gain more than the target), or \emph{neutral}.

\Cref{fig:s2_specialisation} plots target gain against mean non-target gain, and \Cref{tab:s2_specialisation} summarises the verdicts. The headline figures support the hypothesis: 34 of 42 configurations (81.0\%) carry a positive specialisation index, and the mean index is $+0.0313$---architecture-aware prompting systematically biases improvement toward the named planner. Strict specialisation (target up, non-targets down) accounts for 16 configurations (38.1\%) and a further 15 (35.7\%) are universally better, against only 8 (19.0\%) anti-specialised.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{results/s2_specialization_scatter.png}
  \caption{Target-planner \gls{ipc} gain versus mean non-target gain for the 42 improved configurations. Points below the horizontal axis (bottom-right) are strictly specialised---the configuration helps its target while hurting other architectures---and are dominated by \gls{lama}; the high-magnitude points in the upper-right are universally better and come mostly from Depots.}
  \label{fig:s2_specialisation}
\end{figure}

The per-planner pattern (\Cref{tab:s2_specialisation}) is itself architecturally coherent. \gls{lama} produces by far the most strictly specialised configurations (10 of 18, 56\%): because its optimisations exploit order-sensitivity unique to forward search---placing static preconditions first for fast applicability short-circuiting, for instance---they frequently \emph{degrade} planners that read the domain differently. \gls{bfws} carries the highest mean index ($+0.0536$) yet leans universal (6 of 11), and Madagascar is similar: orderings that suit \gls{sat} compilation (goal predicates first, mutex groups adjacent) double as generically clean PDDL that happens to help everyone. The one dissonant case is DecStar, the only planner with a negative mean index ($-0.0079$) and a majority of anti-specialised configurations (4 of 7): its decoupled star-topology decomposition appears to be the architecture whose advantages current \glspl{llm} translate least reliably into reordering rules---an explicit target for the feedback loop and for future prompt engineering.

Two interpretive points temper and strengthen the result in equal measure. First, the universally-better configurations are not a failure of the hypothesis but a by-product of it: in resolving a domain-wide inefficiency for one planner (a goal-critical predicate buried last, say), the model often cleans up structure that was hindering all planners. Indeed, the very largest gains in \Cref{fig:s2_specialisation} are universal and concentrated in Depots, while strict specialisation is associated with more moderate target gains---creating a planner-specific advantage demands delicate structural trade-offs, whereas the biggest wins come from universal cleanup. Second, the thesis claim is precisely the one the data support: not that architecture-aware prompting helps the target \emph{exclusively}, but that it \emph{systematically biases} optimisation toward the target, which the positive mean index and the 81\% majority confirm.

Taken together, Stage~2 delivers the central positive finding of the thesis: supplying an \gls{llm} with planner-architecture knowledge converts the inert reorderings of Stage~1 into improvements that are both frequent (56\% of configurations) and target-biased (81\% positive specialisation index). The boundaries are equally clear---44\% of configurations still fail to improve, DecStar resists specialisation, and twelve planner--domain pairs remain fundamentally unsolvable. Whether an iterative loop that feeds real execution telemetry back to the model can push past these limits is the question taken up by Stage~3 (\Cref{sec:results:stage3}).

\section{Feedback Loop Results (Stage 3)}
\label{sec:results:stage3}

Stage~3 closes the loop. Where Stage~2 produced a single architecture-aware configuration per triple, Stage~3 wraps that configuration in the \gls{llm}-Modulo generate--test--critique cycle of \Cref{sec:bg:llm_modulo}: each Stage~2 domain becomes a \emph{seed}, and for up to three iterations the model is shown a per-instance telemetry table from the target planner---which instances solved, how fast, and at what \gls{ipc} score---and asked to revise its reordering accordingly (\Cref{sec:design:stage3}). Of the 80 (domain, \gls{llm}, planner) triples, the 12 always-timeout triples identified in \Cref{sec:results:stage0} are excluded as uncontestable, leaving 68 contestable triples; the loop executed 218 iterations and 2{,}955 planner runs over roughly 37~hours. The question is whether quantitative execution feedback lets the model surpass its own one-shot Stage~2 result.

\subsection{Iterative Improvement and Failure Recovery}
\label{sec:results:stage3:efficacy}

The feedback loop improved 32 of the 68 contestable triples (47.1\%) beyond their Stage~2 seed. The 36 triples whose seed was never beaten are not loop failures: in most the Stage~2 configuration was already at or near the best reordering reachable within three iterations. Where improvements did occur, the first iteration was the most productive---18 of the 32 best results---which is intuitive, since iteration~1 is the model's first exposure to \emph{any} execution feedback after the blind one-shot Stage~2 prompt, the largest single jump in information. Tellingly, the remaining 14 improvements arrive at iterations~2 and~3, and the convergence profile is non-monotonic: 18 triples improve on the first try, 14 are ``late bloomers'' peaking only at iteration~2 or~3, just 2 improve continuously, and 36 never beat the seed. This shape---together with rationales that visibly shift from generic restatements of the prompt to adaptive self-correction (``I revert to the iteration-1 approach but avoid the action reshuffle that regressed'')---indicates that the loop drives genuine learning from feedback rather than merely buying extra random attempts.

The loop's second contribution is reliability. All five triples that had \emph{failed} in Stage~2---Gemini and GPT-5.4 generations that overran the completion-token limit---were fully recovered (a 100\% recovery rate) once Stage~3 raised the limit and explicitly informed the model of the prior failure through the seed-routing mechanism of \Cref{sec:design:stage3:seed}. These were not marginal saves: the recovered Barman\,$\times$\,Gemini\,$\times$\,\gls{lama} triple reached the maximum possible \gls{ipc} score of 15.00, and Depots\,$\times$\,GPT-5.4\,$\times$\,\gls{bfws} reached 14.58---among the strongest results anywhere in the dataset. The loop thus doubles as a recovery mechanism that converts transient one-shot failures into top-tier configurations.

\subsection{The Combined Result}
\label{sec:results:stage3:combined}

The headline figure of the thesis emerges when the two \gls{llm} stages are scored together against the Stage~0 baseline on a common footing.
Of the 68 contestable triples, the Stage~2 seed alone already beat the baseline on 30 (44.1\%); adding the feedback loop's iterations raises this to 55 (80.9\%), as \Cref{tab:s3_combined} details.
The loop contributes 25 net improvements on top of Stage~2, very nearly doubling the share of configurations that outperform the unmodified domain.\footnote{These baseline-relative rates are computed over the 68 contestable triples against the single globally consistent \gls{ipc} reference (\Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}), and so use a different denominator and a stricter accounting than the within-pipeline screening figure of \Cref{sec:results:stage2}; the two views are reconciled in \Cref{sec:results:cross_stage}.}
The 13 triples that remain unimproved fall into the two categories the earlier stages already anticipated: fundamental planner--domain incompatibilities (the structural floor of \Cref{sec:results:stage0}) and seeds whose Stage~2 reordering was already effectively optimal.

\subsection{Learning Dynamics and Cost}
\label{sec:results:stage3:dynamics}

The population trend conceals sharp differences in how individual models exploit feedback, shown in \Cref{fig:s3_ipc_progression} and summarised in \Cref{tab:s3_llm}. Claude Opus~4.6 and GPT-5.4 learn steadily---GPT-5.4 even accelerates, overtaking the field by iteration~3---whereas Gemini~3.1~Pro peaks at iteration~2 and then regresses, and DeepSeek-R1 is essentially flat across all three rounds. The ability to convert quantitative telemetry into better structure is therefore a genuinely model-dependent capability, not a property of the loop alone.

These curves explain the otherwise surprising re-ordering of the models between stages (\Cref{tab:s3_llm}). Gemini leads Stage~3 (58.8\% of its contestable triples improved), buoyed by three of the five failure recoveries and by the most concise output of any model. Claude, the Stage~2 leader at 65.0\%, falls to 47.1\%: its one-shot seeds were already so strong that little headroom remained, leaving it the smallest mean gain ($+0.10$). DeepSeek is again the outlier---lowest improvement rate (35.3\%) while emitting \num{180741} output tokens, $4.3\times$ GPT-5.4 and Gemini, confirming across both \gls{llm} stages that its chain-of-thought volume is spent rather than invested on what is fundamentally a structural, not a logical, task.

The architectural through-line of the earlier stages persists. Across planners, \gls{lama} and \gls{bfws} improved most under the loop (both 55\%), Madagascar reached 50\%, and DecStar again trailed at 25\%---the same resistance seen in Stage~2 and for the same reason: its star-topology factoring abstracts away the textual ordering the model is manipulating, so the loop helps DecStar mainly by rescuing it from outright failure rather than by incremental tuning. Two further observations bound the result. First, validation quality \emph{declines} across iterations (93.8\%, 90.0\%, 86.8\%) as the accumulating history inflates the prompt and squeezes the output budget; crucially, the hard-critic gate of \Cref{sec:design:validation} caught all 21 invalid iterations before any reached a planner, so no semantically altered domain was ever scored. Second, the per-iteration yield of new best configurations (18, 5, 9) and the degrading validation rate together suggest diminishing returns, making the three-iteration cap a defensible compromise and adaptive stopping a natural item for future work.

Taken together, Stage~3 completes the pipeline: telemetry-driven iteration lifts the baseline-relative improvement rate from 44.1\% to 80.9\%, recovers every Stage~2 failure, and produces measurable population-level learning---while exposing equally clear limits in DecStar, the incompatible planner--domain pairs, and the onset of diminishing returns. \Cref{sec:results:cross_stage} now places all four stages on a single, globally consistent footing.
