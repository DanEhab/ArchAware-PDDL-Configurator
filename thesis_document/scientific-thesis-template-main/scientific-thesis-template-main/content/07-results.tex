\chapter{Results}\label{ch:results}
% Pure results presentation and interpretation — no design or methodology

The present chapter reports the empirical results of the four-stage configuration pipeline designed in \Cref{ch:design} and parameterised in \Cref{ch:experimental_setup}.
Every stage is evaluated against the same two primary outcome measures.
The first is \emph{coverage}: the number of benchmark instances a planner solves within the 360-second limit.
The second is the \emph{\gls{ipc} score} of \Cref{eq:ipc_score}, a normalised per-instance efficiency measure that rewards fast solutions and ranges from~0 (unsolved) to~1 (matching the best observed time).

The chapter follows the logical progression of the pipeline.
\Cref{sec:results:stage0} characterises the unmodified baseline (Stage~0), establishing the reference against which later improvements are measured.
\Cref{sec:results:stage1,sec:results:stage2,sec:results:stage3} then report the three configuration stages in turn---the general prompt, the architecture-aware prompt, and the feedback loop.
\Cref{sec:results:cross_stage} synthesises the full progression, \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation} provides the statistical analysis, and \Cref{sec:results:prior_work} positions the findings relative to the prior work of \citeauthor{Elis2025}~\cite{Elis2025} and \citeauthor{Georgievski2025}~\cite{Georgievski2025}.

\section{Baseline Characterisation (Stage 0)}
\label{sec:results:stage0}

Stage~0 executes the four target planners on the five original, unmodified \gls{ipc} domains, with no \gls{llm} in the loop.
As set out in \Cref{sec:design:stage0}, Stage~0 measures how each planner performs on the unmodified domains---the performance that every later stage is compared against---and characterises the difficulty landscape: which domains are intrinsically hard, which planners struggle, and where there is genuine room for configuration to help.
The full factorial design comprises $4~\text{planners} \times 5~\text{domains} \times 15~\text{instances} = 300$ runs.

\subsection{Aggregate Execution Outcome}
\label{sec:results:stage0:aggregate}

The complete baseline sweep finished in 5~hours 14~minutes of wall-clock pipeline time.
Of the 300 runs, 168 (56.0\%) terminated in \texttt{SUCCESS} and the remaining 132 (44.0\%) in \texttt{TIMEOUT}.
Critically, \emph{no run produced a \texttt{MEMOUT} or \texttt{FAILURE}}: under the 8~GB memory cap, every planner either solved an instance within the time limit or exhausted that limit while still searching.
This clean, binary outcome is methodologically reassuring---it confirms that the resource envelope (\Cref{sec:setup:hyperparameters}) was correctly calibrated and that the 132 timeouts reflect genuine search intractability rather than crashes, parser rejections, or out-of-memory faults that would otherwise contaminate the reference baseline.

\Cref{tab:s0_planner_summary} summarises performance by planner.
Across the 168 successful runs, the mean wall-clock time was 48.88~s but the median only 8.47~s, with individual runs ranging from 0.41~s to 351.23~s.
This more-than-fivefold gap between mean and median exposes the heavy right-skew that characterises planning runtimes: a small minority of hard-but-solved instances dominates the arithmetic mean.
The skew is not a nuisance to be smoothed away---it is the empirical reason why every significance test in this chapter relies on the non-parametric, paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test rather than a mean-based parametric alternative (\Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}).

\subsection{Planner-Level Performance}
\label{sec:results:stage0:planners}

The four planners separate into two clear tiers.
\gls{bfws} solves 85.33\% of instances (64/75) and \gls{lama} 70.67\% (53/75), whereas DecStar and Madagascar solve only 34.67\% (26/75) and 33.33\% (25/75) respectively---less than half the coverage of the leading pair.
\gls{bfws}'s lead is consistent with its width-based search paradigm (\Cref{sec:setup:planners}).
Rather than relying on a single goal-distance heuristic, which may be poorly informed on unfamiliar domains, \gls{bfws} prioritises states that exhibit structurally \emph{novel} combinations of variable assignments; as the \gls{ipc}~2018 Agile-track winner, it is explicitly tuned to find \emph{any} valid plan quickly, an objective that aligns directly with this study's time-limited setting.
Madagascar, the only \gls{sat}-based planner, occupies the opposite extreme.
It translates each planning task into a sequence of Boolean formulae---one per candidate plan length---and delegates them to a \gls{sat} solver.
When a short plan exists this is extraordinarily fast (Madagascar is the quickest planner on Depots, mean 0.58~s), but when the optimal horizon is long or unknown the solver must iterate over ever-larger formulae, which explains why Madagascar fails outright on the long-horizon VisitAll and Ricochet Robots domains.

Two columns of \Cref{tab:s0_planner_summary} look paradoxical at first: \gls{bfws} records the \emph{highest} mean wall-clock time (66.98~s) despite being the strongest planner, and DecStar and Madagascar record the \emph{lowest} mean plan costs (118.7 and 97.3) despite being the weakest.
Both effects have the same cause: wall-clock time, plan cost, and states expanded are recorded only for \texttt{SUCCESS} runs, so each planner's averages are taken over a \emph{different} set of instances.
\gls{bfws} and \gls{lama} solve the long-horizon VisitAll instances---whose plans run to several thousand actions---and those instances inflate both their mean runtimes and their mean costs, whereas the weaker planners time out on them and are averaged only over the short, easy instances they do solve.
Coverage is therefore the only planner-level aggregate in \Cref{tab:s0_planner_summary} that can be compared across planners; every efficiency metric must instead be compared within a fixed (domain, instance) cell---the reason for the per-instance \gls{ipc}-score pairing used throughout the rest of the chapter.

When a like-for-like comparison \emph{is} made, \gls{bfws}'s efficiency advantage is clear.
On Ricochet Robots, for example, it expands far fewer states than its rivals on the same instances---a mean of \num{95825}, against \gls{lama}'s \num{1380370} and DecStar's \num{530587}---confirming that its novelty heuristic prunes the search space more aggressively than delete-relaxation heuristics or decoupled search.

\subsection{Domain Difficulty Landscape}
\label{sec:results:stage0:domains}

The domains differ just as sharply, as \Cref{tab:s0_domain_difficulty} shows.
Depots is almost trivially easy (98.3\% solved; 59 of 60 runs), whereas Snake is the hardest (31.7\%), with Ricochet Robots close behind (36.7\%).
This ranking follows directly from each domain's structure.
Depots (IPC~2002) is a well-studied logistics domain with a strongly typed three-level hierarchy and spatially independent sub-problems, properties that every architecture exploits effectively.
Snake (IPC~2018), by contrast, is untyped, relies on negative preconditions, and is dense with irreversible dead-end states: a single wrong move can render the goal unreachable, forcing planners to waste effort exploring doomed regions of the state space.
Ricochet Robots (IPC~2023) is hard for a different reason---its sliding-block mechanic produces long action sequences and demands that pieces be coordinated as mutual blockers, a structure that defeats most goal-distance heuristics.
The 67-percentage-point gap between the easiest and hardest domain confirms that the five-domain selection of \Cref{sec:setup:domains} spans the intended breadth of structural difficulty.

\subsection{The Planner--Domain Coverage Interaction}
\label{sec:results:stage0:coverage}

Neither the planner view nor the domain view is sufficient on its own; what matters is their \emph{interaction}, shown in the coverage heatmap of \Cref{fig:s0_coverage_heatmap} and as runtimes in \Cref{tab:s0_walltime}.
Three features of this matrix recur throughout the later stages.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{results/s0_coverage_heatmap.png}
  \caption{Stage~0 coverage matrix: instances solved (out of 15) for each (domain, planner) pair. \gls{bfws} (leftmost column) attains the highest or joint-highest coverage in every one of the five domains. The three pale cells mark architecturally incompatible pairs that never solve a single instance, while the saturated Depots row shows a domain that every planner finds near-trivial.}
  \label{fig:s0_coverage_heatmap}
\end{figure}

First, \gls{bfws} attains the highest or joint-highest coverage in \emph{every} domain---15/15 on VisitAll, Depots, and Barman, and the single best result on the two hardest domains (8/15 on Snake, 11/15 on Ricochet Robots).
No other planner is so uniformly robust, which makes \gls{bfws} the natural reference architecture for the coverage dimension.

Second, three (domain, planner) cells are \emph{completely unsolvable}: VisitAll$\times$DecStar, VisitAll$\times$Madagascar, and Ricochet Robots$\times$Madagascar each score 0/15.
These zeros are not noise but architectural incompatibilities.
VisitAll's single agent and fully connected grid offer no independent sub-systems for DecStar's star-topology decomposition to exploit, so its factoring overhead yields only cost; and both unsolved domains require the long action horizons that are pathological for Madagascar's length-indexed \gls{sat} encoding.
These three pairs reappear in Stage~3 as the twelve always-timeout triples (three pairs across the four \glspl{llm}) that are excluded from the contestable-improvement analysis, since merely reordering the text of a domain cannot make a planner solve one that its search paradigm is fundamentally unsuited to.

Third, seven cells are \emph{saturated} at 15/15, but they are not equally interesting as optimisation targets.
\Cref{tab:s0_walltime} resolves them by runtime.
Four are already \emph{trivially fast}---Depots under Madagascar (0.58~s), \gls{lama} (1.99~s), and \gls{bfws} (3.13~s), and Barman under \gls{lama} (3.62~s)---leaving essentially no runtime headroom, so that any Stage~1--3 gain on these cells can come only from plan-quality improvements.
The other three saturated cells (VisitAll$\times$\gls{bfws} at 17.6~s, VisitAll$\times$\gls{lama} at 44.0~s, and Barman$\times$\gls{bfws} at 53.2~s) solve every instance but take appreciably longer than the trivially fast cells, and therefore leave runtime that a better-structured domain could potentially recover.

Together, these features sort the (domain, planner) cells into three groups that recur in the later stages: \emph{saturated-fast} cells with little runtime left to recover; \emph{zero-coverage} cells that reordering alone is unlikely to fix; and a broad \emph{contested middle}---the partially solved cells of Snake, Ricochet Robots, and the weaker planners on Barman and VisitAll---where both coverage and runtime can realistically change. Most of the room for improvement in the later stages lies in this middle group.

\subsection{Plan Cost, Runtime Variance, and Measurement Scope}
\label{sec:results:stage0:cost}

Three measurement lessons from the baseline shape how the later stages must be compared. \Cref{fig:s0_runtime_cost} plots wall-clock time against plan cost for all 168 successful runs and makes the first of them visible.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{results/s0_runtime_cost_scatter.png}
  \caption{Per-run wall-clock time (log scale) versus plan cost for all 168 successful baseline runs, coloured by planner and shaped by domain. The isolated upper cluster of high-cost plans belongs exclusively to \gls{bfws} and \gls{lama} on VisitAll; because DecStar and Madagascar never solve that domain, their absence from this region---not any intrinsic plan-quality advantage---explains their deceptively low mean plan costs.}
  \label{fig:s0_runtime_cost}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{First, plan cost is comparable only within a domain.}
The isolated cluster of very long plans (2{,}800--5{,}300 actions) in \Cref{fig:s0_runtime_cost} consists entirely of \gls{bfws} and \gls{lama} runs on VisitAll---the one domain that demands such long plans, and one the weaker planners never solve.
Within any single domain the gap closes: on Depots all four planners produce 48.7--61.8-action plans, and on Barman 197--293.
The low average plan costs of DecStar and Madagascar in \Cref{tab:s0_planner_summary} therefore reflect which instances they happened to solve, not better solution quality.

\paragraph{Second, runtime varies widely even within a single cell.}
Even for one (domain, planner) pair, solve times differ enormously from instance to instance: in the most extreme case, Depots$\times$DecStar, the easiest instance solves in 0.86~s and the hardest in 53.04~s.
Comparing the \emph{average} solve time of a baseline against a configured domain would therefore be dominated by which instances happen to be involved, which is why every later comparison scores each instance against its own baseline counterpart rather than comparing averages.

\paragraph{Third, the planners do not report the same telemetry.}
Madagascar (\gls{sat}-based) reports no state-space counts at all, and \gls{bfws} omits state-evaluation and peak-memory figures (\Cref{sec:impl:planners}); only plan cost and wall-clock runtime are available for all four.
Because the \gls{ipc} score is derived from wall-clock runtime and the solved/unsolved outcome alone, it is the one efficiency measure that can be computed identically for every planner---the reason it is used for all the cross-architecture comparisons that follow.

\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:results:stage0:synthesis}

The baseline provides what the later stages need.
It records how each planner performs on the unmodified domains---the performance every later stage is measured against (\Cref{tab:s0_walltime}).
It marks out where improvement is possible, separating the twelve always-timeout triples and the four trivially fast cells, where little or no runtime can be recovered, from the broad contested middle where improvement is genuinely possible.
And it exposes the planner-specific behaviour that the architecture-aware prompts of \Cref{sec:setup:arch_aware_prompt} are designed to exploit: \gls{bfws}'s novelty-driven state economy, Madagascar's affinity for short-horizon \gls{sat} encodings, DecStar's dependence on decomposable sub-systems, and \gls{lama}'s balanced heuristic search.
With the baseline established, \Cref{sec:results:stage1} turns to the first intervention---the general-purpose \gls{llm} prompt of Stage~1.

\section{General Prompt Results (Stage 1)}
\label{sec:results:stage1}

Stage~1 introduces the first \gls{llm} intervention: the generic, planner-agnostic prompt of \Cref{sec:setup:general_prompt} (the $\Pi_\text{ZS-S}$ zero-shot-short strategy), which simply asks each model to reorder a domain ``to improve AI planner efficiency'' without naming or describing any target planner.
Each of the four \glspl{llm} processed all five domains, producing 20 candidate domains; those that passed validation were executed across all four planners and 15 instances, for 1{,}080 planner runs.
Because the prompt withholds all architectural knowledge, this stage functions as a controlled reference point for the configuration idea itself, and its guiding question is deliberately binary: \emph{does generic reordering, on its own, move planner performance?}

\subsection{Generation and Validation}
\label{sec:results:stage1:generation}

All 20 \gls{llm} API calls returned a complete response without error, and every response passed extraction (V1) and VAL syntax checking (V2) of the four-level validation pipeline (\Cref{sec:design:validation}).
Two outputs were rejected at the identity check (V3)---both from DeepSeek-R1, on Ricochet Robots and Barman---leaving 18 of 20 candidates (90\%) valid, all of which passed the semantic-equivalence check (V4) intact (\Cref{tab:s1_generation}).
The perfect syntax and semantic rates are themselves a finding: when instructed to emit only valid PDDL and to preserve meaning, no model introduced a syntax error or an illegal logical change. The models reliably separate reorderable surface form from load-bearing semantics.

The two rejections expose a behavioural axis among the models. DeepSeek-R1, whose reasoning architecture deliberates before answering, judged two domains already well-ordered and returned them verbatim; the identity guard (V3) rejects such outputs because they are indistinguishable from the Stage~0 baseline. The same conservatism is visible in the reordering effort of \Cref{tab:s1_generation}: DeepSeek-R1 made only 9 individual reorderings, against 28--33 for the other three models, with Claude Opus~4.6 the most active. This caution carries no efficiency benefit---DeepSeek-R1 emitted \num{10201} output tokens, roughly three times its peers, as its chain-of-thought trace inflates the completion---making it simultaneously the most expensive and the least productive model at this stage.

\subsection{What the Models Chose to Reorder}
\label{sec:results:stage1:reordering}

\Cref{fig:s1_reordering} shows how the 18 valid reorderings distribute across the nine PDDL component types.
The edits concentrate sharply on \emph{operational} components---preconditions (modified in 16 of 18 domains), actions (12), add-effects (11), delete-effects (6), and predicates (4)---while four components are \emph{never} touched: requirements, types, functions, and parameters.
Both ends of this distribution are informative.
The leading order---preconditions before actions before effects---matches the intuition of the planning literature: precondition order governs early applicability pruning during successor generation, and action order governs the sequence in which operators are first considered.
The four zeros are arguably the stronger result.
Requirements, types, and functions are declarative sections parsed once at initialisation, with no bearing on search; and parameter order is semantically load-bearing, since permuting an action's parameters would invalidate every problem file that references it.
That all four models avoided these components---parameters above all---corroborates the V4 outcome from a second direction: the reorderings are safe and operationally targeted, not random shuffling.
Absent planner-specific guidance, the models converge on the same generic intuition about what matters; they differ only in degree, not in kind.

\subsection{Planner Impact: A Controlled Null Result}
\label{sec:results:stage1:impact}

Valid and sensible as these edits are, they leave planner performance essentially unchanged.
The Stage~1 overall solve rate is 56.57\% (611/1{,}080), against the Stage~0 baseline of 56.00\%---a difference of barely half a percentage point.
\Cref{tab:s1_solve_rate} confirms that the stability holds planner by planner: no planner moves by more than 3.5 percentage points, the largest shifts being Madagascar's $+3.34$ and \gls{bfws}'s $-2.00$.
The timeout landscape is likewise frozen in place: the hardest domains (Snake, Ricochet Robots) and the most timeout-prone planners (DecStar, Madagascar) remain exactly those identified in \Cref{sec:results:stage0}, each within about two percentage points of its baseline rate.
Coverage is also largely insensitive to \emph{which} model produced the domain: the saturated cells (Depots\,$\times$\,\gls{bfws} at 15/15) and the incompatible cells (VisitAll\,$\times$\,DecStar at 0/15) are identical across all four \glspl{llm}, and the partially solved cells differ by at most one or two instances (for instance, \gls{bfws} on Snake solves 7--8 instances depending on the model).

The near-perfect stability is, in one sense, exactly what a semantics-preserving transformation should produce: an instance that is fundamentally unsolvable or already trivial at baseline remains so after its components are merely reshuffled.
But the same stability is the central negative result of the stage---generic reordering does not shift the difficulty landscape.
Coverage is admittedly a coarse lens, and small per-instance runtime changes can still occur within an unchanged coverage envelope; those finer \gls{ipc}-score effects are quantified in the cross-stage analysis (\Cref{sec:results:cross_stage}).
At the level of what a planner-agnostic prompt can \emph{reliably} achieve, however, the conclusion is unambiguous: instructing a model that a domain should be ``more efficient'' is not the same as telling it how a particular planner will read that domain.
Closing precisely this gap---by injecting planner-architecture knowledge into the prompt---is the task of Stage~2 (\Cref{sec:results:stage2}).

\section{Architecture-Aware Prompt Results (Stage 2)}
\label{sec:results:stage2}

Stage~2 replaces the planner-agnostic prompt of Stage~1 with the four planner-tailored, architecture-aware prompts of \Cref{sec:setup:arch_aware_prompt}, each of which injects a synthesised description of one target planner's internal pipeline together with planner-specific reordering rules.
Every \gls{llm} now produces a separate configuration for each planner, so the design expands to $4~\text{\glspl{llm}} \times 5~\text{domains} \times 4~\text{planner prompts} = 80$ generations.
The stage answers two questions in sequence.
The first is one of \emph{efficacy}: does naming the target planner and describing how it reads a domain yield measurable improvements where the generic prompt did not?
The second, and the one that bears most directly on the thesis hypothesis, is one of \emph{specialisation}: are those improvements genuinely tied to the planner they were designed for, or do they merely make the domain better for everyone?
A dedicated cross-testing phase (\Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}) is what makes the second question answerable.

\subsection{Generation and Validation}
\label{sec:results:stage2:generation}

Of the 80 generations, 75 (93.8\%) passed validation. The five losses split into three Gemini~3.1~Pro outputs that exceeded the completion-token limit on the longest prompts (the DecStar and \gls{lama} prompts for Depots and Barman) and two GPT-5.4 outputs rejected at the semantic check~(V4)---on the \gls{lama} prompt for Ricochet Robots and the \gls{bfws} prompt for Depots---where the model went beyond reordering and altered a precondition or effect in its attempt to optimise. Claude Opus~4.6 again validated on all 20 calls.

More revealing than the pass rate is \emph{what} changed. Under the architecture-aware prompts the reordering of predicate declarations jumps to 75 of 75 valid domains (100\%), against just 22\% under the generic prompt of Stage~1 (\Cref{fig:s1_reordering}). This is the clearest behavioural fingerprint that the architectural guidance was actually absorbed: all four prompts name predicate ordering as the highest-priority lever (for mutex discovery, novelty-table indexing, or SAT variable assignment), and the models obeyed uniformly. Add-effects (87\%), preconditions (85\%), and actions (75\%) follow, while parameters (1\%) and the declarative sections (0\%) remain untouched---the same safe, operational-only discipline observed in Stage~1. As in earlier stages, DeepSeek-R1's reasoning trace inflated its output to roughly \num{106185} tokens (about seven times GPT-5.4 and Claude), with no corresponding advantage in outcome.

\subsection{Improvement Efficacy}
\label{sec:results:stage2:efficacy}

Each of the 75 validated configurations was executed on its target planner (1{,}125 runs) and evaluated by the three-condition improvement test of \Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}, which requires a statistically significant, practically positive, and coverage-preserving gain in per-instance \gls{ipc} score over the Stage~0 baseline.
\emph{Forty-two of the 75 configurations (56.0\%) qualified as improved}---a decisive departure from the inert generic reorderings of Stage~1.
Because this gate is the pipeline's internal screening step, it applies the relaxed $\alpha = 0.25$ threshold motivated in \Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}; the confirmatory analysis at the conventional $\alpha = 0.05$ is reported separately in \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation}.

The single most important result of the stage is \emph{how} that improvement rate distributes across the four target planners (\Cref{tab:s2_improvement_planner}).
The ordering---\gls{lama} (100\%) $>$ \gls{bfws} (57.9\%) $>$ DecStar (38.9\%) $>$ Madagascar (30.0\%)---tracks almost exactly how sensitive each architecture is to the textual ordering of a domain.
\gls{lama}'s entire forward-search pipeline is order-driven: predicate order shapes mutex and landmark discovery, and precondition order determines which supports the FF heuristic selects as preferred operators, so every one of its 18 configurations improved.
Madagascar sits at the opposite pole: it compiles the task to \gls{sat}, and the solver's dynamic variable-ordering heuristics (such as VSIDS) rapidly override whatever initial variable order the PDDL layout imposes, washing out most of the \gls{llm}'s structural intent.
\gls{bfws} and DecStar fall in between, consistent with their partial reliance on textual structure.
This monotonic relationship between architectural order-sensitivity and responsiveness to configuration is, in miniature, the empirical core of the thesis.

The reorderings make planners solve the \emph{same} instances faster.

\subsection{Architecture Specialisation}
\label{sec:results:stage2:specialisation}

Establishing that the prompts \emph{improve} the target planner is only half the claim; the thesis asserts that the improvements are \emph{specific} to that planner. To test this, every one of the 42 improved domains was re-executed on the three planners it was \emph{not} designed for---1{,}890 additional cross-test runs. For each configuration we define its \emph{specialisation index} as the target-planner \gls{ipc} gain minus the mean gain across the three non-target planners, computed on a per-instance local reference so that target and non-target cells are numerically comparable. A positive index means the optimisation favoured its intended planner. Each configuration is then classed as \emph{specialised} (non-targets are actively hurt, by more than a $0.005$ noise margin), \emph{universally better} (non-targets also gain, but no more than the target), \emph{anti-specialised} (non-targets gain more than the target), or \emph{neutral}.

\Cref{fig:s2_specialisation} plots target gain against mean non-target gain, and \Cref{tab:s2_specialisation} summarises the verdicts. The headline figures support the hypothesis: 34 of 42 configurations (81.0\%) carry a positive specialisation index, and the mean index is $+0.0313$---architecture-aware prompting systematically biases improvement toward the named planner. Strict specialisation (target up, non-targets down) accounts for 16 configurations (38.1\%) and a further 15 (35.7\%) are universally better, against only 8 (19.0\%) anti-specialised.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{results/s2_specialization_scatter.png}
  \caption{Target-planner \gls{ipc} gain versus mean non-target gain for the 42 improved configurations. Points below the horizontal axis (bottom-right) are strictly specialised---the configuration helps its target while hurting other architectures---and are dominated by \gls{lama}; the high-magnitude points in the upper-right are universally better and come mostly from Depots.}
  \label{fig:s2_specialisation}
\end{figure}

The per-planner pattern (\Cref{tab:s2_specialisation}) is itself architecturally coherent. \gls{lama} produces by far the most strictly specialised configurations (10 of 18, 56\%): because its optimisations exploit order-sensitivity unique to forward search---placing static preconditions first for fast applicability short-circuiting, for instance---they frequently \emph{degrade} planners that read the domain differently. \gls{bfws} carries the highest mean index ($+0.0536$) yet leans universal (6 of 11), and Madagascar is similar: orderings that suit \gls{sat} compilation (goal predicates first, mutex groups adjacent) double as generically clean PDDL that happens to help everyone. The one dissonant case is DecStar, the only planner with a negative mean index ($-0.0079$) and a majority of anti-specialised configurations (4 of 7): its decoupled star-topology decomposition appears to be the architecture whose advantages current \glspl{llm} translate least reliably into reordering rules---an explicit target for the feedback loop and for future prompt engineering.

Two interpretive points temper and strengthen the result in equal measure. First, the universally-better configurations are not a failure of the hypothesis but a by-product of it: in resolving a domain-wide inefficiency for one planner (a goal-critical predicate buried last, say), the model often cleans up structure that was hindering all planners. Indeed, the very largest gains in \Cref{fig:s2_specialisation} are universal and concentrated in Depots, while strict specialisation is associated with more moderate target gains---creating a planner-specific advantage demands delicate structural trade-offs, whereas the biggest wins come from universal cleanup. Second, the thesis claim is precisely the one the data support: not that architecture-aware prompting helps the target \emph{exclusively}, but that it \emph{systematically biases} optimisation toward the target, which the positive mean index and the 81\% majority confirm.

Taken together, Stage~2 delivers the central positive finding of the thesis: supplying an \gls{llm} with planner-architecture knowledge converts the inert reorderings of Stage~1 into improvements that are both frequent (56\% of configurations) and target-biased (81\% positive specialisation index). The boundaries are equally clear---44\% of configurations still fail to improve, DecStar resists specialisation, and twelve planner--domain--model triples remain fundamentally unsolvable. Whether an iterative loop that feeds real execution telemetry back to the model can push past these limits is the question taken up by Stage~3 (\Cref{sec:results:stage3}).

\section{Feedback Loop Results (Stage 3)}
\label{sec:results:stage3}

Stage~3 closes the loop. Where Stage~2 produced a single architecture-aware configuration per triple, Stage~3 wraps that configuration in the \gls{llm}-Modulo generate--test--critique cycle of \Cref{sec:bg:llm_modulo}: each Stage~2 domain becomes a \emph{seed}, and for up to three iterations the model is shown a per-instance telemetry table from the target planner---which instances solved, how fast, and at what \gls{ipc} score---and asked to revise its reordering accordingly (\Cref{sec:design:stage3}). All 80 (domain, \gls{llm}, planner) triples enter the loop, but the 12 always-timeout triples identified in \Cref{sec:results:stage0} terminate after a single iteration---no reordering can make an all-timeout cell solvable---and are excluded from the contestable analysis, leaving 68 contestable triples; in total the loop executed 218 iterations and 2{,}955 planner runs over roughly 37~hours. The question is whether quantitative execution feedback lets the model surpass its own one-shot Stage~2 result.

\subsection{Iterative Improvement and Failure Recovery}
\label{sec:results:stage3:efficacy}

The feedback loop improved 32 of the 68 contestable triples (47.1\%) beyond their Stage~2 seed. The 36 triples whose seed was never beaten are not loop failures: in most the Stage~2 configuration was already at or near the best reordering reachable within three iterations. Where improvements did occur, the first iteration was the most productive---18 of the 32 best results---which is intuitive, since iteration~1 is the model's first exposure to \emph{any} execution feedback after the blind one-shot Stage~2 prompt, the largest single jump in information. Tellingly, the remaining 14 improvements arrive at iterations~2 and~3, and the convergence profile is non-monotonic: 18 triples improve on the first try, 14 are ``late bloomers'' peaking only at iteration~2 or~3, just 2 improve continuously, and 36 never beat the seed. This shape---together with rationales that visibly shift from generic restatements of the prompt to adaptive self-correction (``I revert to the iteration-1 approach but avoid the action reshuffle that regressed'')---indicates that the loop drives genuine learning from feedback rather than merely buying extra random attempts.

The loop's second contribution is reliability. All five triples that had \emph{failed} in Stage~2---Gemini and GPT-5.4 generations that overran the completion-token limit---were fully recovered (a 100\% recovery rate) once Stage~3 raised the limit and explicitly informed the model of the prior failure through the seed-routing mechanism of \Cref{sec:design:stage3:seed}. These were not marginal saves: the recovered Barman\,$\times$\,Gemini\,$\times$\,\gls{lama} triple reached the maximum possible \gls{ipc} score of 15.00, and Depots\,$\times$\,GPT-5.4\,$\times$\,\gls{bfws} reached 14.58---among the strongest results anywhere in the dataset. The loop thus doubles as a recovery mechanism that converts transient one-shot failures into top-tier configurations.

\subsection{The Combined Result}
\label{sec:results:stage3:combined}

The headline figure of the thesis emerges when the two \gls{llm} stages are scored together against the Stage~0 baseline on a common footing.
Of the 68 contestable triples, the Stage~2 seed alone already beat the baseline on 30 (44.1\%); adding the feedback loop's iterations raises this to 55 (80.9\%), as \Cref{tab:s3_combined} details.
The loop contributes 25 net improvements on top of Stage~2, very nearly doubling the share of configurations that outperform the unmodified domain.\footnote{These baseline-relative rates are computed over the 68 contestable triples against the single globally consistent \gls{ipc} reference (\Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}), and so use a different denominator and a stricter accounting than the within-pipeline screening figure of \Cref{sec:results:stage2}; the two views are reconciled in \Cref{sec:results:cross_stage}.}
The 13 triples that remain unimproved fall into the two categories the earlier stages already anticipated: fundamental planner--domain incompatibilities (the structural floor of \Cref{sec:results:stage0}) and seeds whose Stage~2 reordering was already effectively optimal.

The architectural through-line of the earlier stages persists. Across planners, \gls{lama} and \gls{bfws} improved most under the loop (both 55\%), Madagascar reached 50\%, and DecStar again trailed at 25\%---the same resistance seen in Stage~2 and for the same reason: its star-topology factoring abstracts away the textual ordering the model is manipulating, so the loop helps DecStar mainly by rescuing it from outright failure rather than by incremental tuning. Two further observations bound the result. First, validation quality declines across the iterations as the accumulating history inflates the prompt and squeezes the output budget (the per-iteration rates and the hard-critic guarantee are consolidated in \Cref{sec:results:cross_stage:validation}). Second, this degradation, together with the falling per-iteration yield of new best configurations (18, 5, 9), suggests diminishing returns, making the three-iteration cap a defensible compromise and adaptive stopping a natural item for future work.

Taken together, Stage~3 completes the pipeline: telemetry-driven iteration lifts the baseline-relative improvement rate from 44.1\% to 80.9\%, recovers every Stage~2 failure, and produces measurable population-level learning---while exposing equally clear limits in DecStar, the incompatible planner--domain pairs, and the onset of diminishing returns. \Cref{sec:results:cross_stage} now places all four stages on a single, globally consistent footing.

\section{Cross-Stage Comparative Analysis}
\label{sec:results:cross_stage}

The preceding four sections each scored a stage against the baseline using the within-pipeline screening of \Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}. This section instead places all four stages on a single, globally consistent footing: every one of the 7{,}350 planner runs is re-scored against one reference time $T^*_i$ per instance---the fastest wall-clock time \emph{any} configuration of \emph{any} stage achieved on that instance (\Cref{eq:ipc_score}). Two reference scopes are reported throughout, answering complementary questions. \emph{Configuration Sensitivity} fixes $T^*_i$ per planner, isolating the effect of configuration on each planner in turn (``did reordering help this planner?''). \emph{Simulated Competition} fixes a single $T^*_i$ across all planners, reproducing a true competition in which every configuration competes against the global best (``which stage produced the best configurations overall?''). Re-scoring on this common basis both consolidates the four-stage progression and confirms that it is not an artefact of the relaxed screening threshold used earlier.

\label{sec:results:cross_stage:progression}

This is the universal-transfer effect of \Cref{sec:results:stage2:specialisation} seen from the competition side---architecture-aware reorderings, though target-biased, frequently encode general structural improvements that benefit other architectures too.

\subsection{Coverage and Runtime}
\label{sec:results:cross_stage:coverage}

\gls{ipc} score folds speed and coverage into one number; separating them confirms that the pipeline moves both. On coverage, the portfolio of \gls{llm} configurations unlocks 15 of the 132 instances that timed out at baseline (an 11.4\% reduction of the unsolvable frontier), while the single-configuration hit rate rises from 56.0\% to 62.9\% across the stages and regressions are near-zero (no portfolio cell lost coverage at Stage~2). As \Cref{tab:cs_coverage_par10} shows, both gains are concentrated exactly where architecture predicts: Madagascar---whose \gls{sat} encoding is most helped by structural alignment---unlocks the most coverage ($+13.33$ percentage points) and sheds the most \gls{par10} penalty (from \num{2411} to \num{1944}\,s), whereas DecStar is immovable on both, its factored search neutralising the reorderings entirely.

The generic stage is again the outlier: on the same commonly-solved instances it \emph{slows} Madagascar by 32\% and the Barman domain by 23\%, before Stages~2 and~3 reverse the loss. This is the cross-stage echo of the Stage~1 null result---generic reordering is not merely unhelpful but occasionally harmful, and only planner-aware reordering reliably accelerates search.

\subsection{Validation Integrity Across the Pipeline}
\label{sec:results:cross_stage:validation}

A premise of every result above---and the guarantee that addresses \textbf{SQ4}---is that no semantically altered domain was ever scored. Across the three \gls{llm} stages the four-level validation pipeline (\Cref{sec:design:validation}) upheld this line. Validity was high at the one-shot stages, at 90.0\% for Stage~1 (18 of 20 candidates) and 93.8\% for Stage~2 (75 of 80); and although it declined across the feedback iterations (93.8\%, 90.0\%, 86.8\%) as the growing history inflated the prompt, the hard-critic gate rejected all 21 invalid Stage~3 iterations before any reached a planner. In every case the rejections were genuine catches rather than false alarms---verbatim-copy returns at V3, illegal semantic edits at V4, and token-limit overruns---so not a single semantically modified domain entered the performance measurements, and every efficiency gain reported in this chapter reflects a change in element \emph{ordering alone}.

\subsection{Synthesis}
\label{sec:results:cross_stage:synthesis}

Viewed through every lens---aggregate \gls{ipc} score, source of best configuration, coverage frontier, and \gls{par10}---the four stages tell one consistent story. The continuous \gls{ipc} progression ($154.17 \rightarrow 163.87$) corroborates the discrete improvement-rate progression ($44.1\% \rightarrow 80.9\%$) of \Cref{sec:results:stage3}: the two accountings, computed on entirely different bases, agree that each stage adds value and that the pipeline as a whole beats the baseline on essentially every solvable instance. The dominant axis of variation is architectural, not lexical: responsiveness ranks \gls{bfws}~$>$~\gls{lama}~$>$~Madagascar~$>$~DecStar under every metric, and the methodology widens rather than narrows that ranking. Finally, the ablation locates the value precisely---architecture-awareness (Stage~2) is the decisive single intervention, generic prompting (Stage~1) is marginal-to-harmful, and the feedback loop (Stage~3) trades steeply rising cost for breadth and reliability. What remains is to establish that this progression is statistically real rather than a favourable reading of noisy data, which is the task of \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation}.

\section{Statistical Validation}
\label{sec:results:statistical_validation}

The progression of \Cref{sec:results:cross_stage} is, so far, a description of point estimates. This section subjects it to formal hypothesis testing to establish that the stage differences are real rather than a favourable reading of noisy data. All tests operate on the 300 paired per-instance \gls{ipc} observations---the same $(\text{planner}, \text{domain}, \text{instance})$ cells measured under all four stages (best portfolio, Configuration Sensitivity)---which makes the design fully paired. Whereas the within-pipeline screening of \Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement} deliberately used a relaxed $\alpha = 0.25$ to admit candidate configurations, every test reported here uses the conventional confirmatory thresholds.

\subsection{Test Selection}
\label{sec:results:statistical_validation:selection}

The choice of test family is dictated by the data. A Shapiro--Wilk test rejects normality for all four stage distributions and all three gain distributions, decisively in every case ($W$ between 0.38 and 0.69; every $p < 10^{-22}$). This is the expected consequence of two structural features of \gls{ipc} scores: a spike of exact zeros from unsolved instances, and the hard $[0,1]$ bound. Parametric procedures (the paired $t$-test, repeated-measures ANOVA) are therefore inadmissible, and the entire suite below is rank-based: the Friedman omnibus test, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for pairwise contrasts (Bonferroni-corrected to $\alpha = 0.05/6 = 0.0083$ for the six stage pairs), the Nemenyi post-hoc test, Cliff's~$\delta$ for effect sizes, and Kruskal--Wallis tests for the categorical factors.

\subsection{The Stage Effect}
\label{sec:results:statistical_validation:stage}

The Friedman test rejects the null hypothesis that the four stages share a common rank distribution with overwhelming margin: $\chi^2 = 267.06$, $p = 1.3 \times 10^{-57}$, Kendall's $W = 0.30$. The mean ranks order the stages $\text{S0}\,(1.90) < \text{S1}\,(2.18) < \text{S3}\,(2.88) < \text{S2}\,(3.04)$, placing the architecture-aware stage marginally above the feedback loop and both well above the generic and baseline stages.

The pairwise structure behind this omnibus result is given in \Cref{tab:stat_pairwise}, with the corresponding effect sizes and bootstrap confidence intervals in \Cref{fig:stat_forest}. Three conclusions follow. First, \emph{every} configuration stage significantly outperforms the baseline; the comparison is starkest for Stage~2, where 174 of the 181 instances with a non-zero difference improve and only 7 worsen ($p = 1.4 \times 10^{-29}$). Second, and most importantly for the thesis, Stage~2 significantly beats Stage~1 ($158$ improved versus $24$ worsened, $p = 2.2 \times 10^{-21}$, Cliff's $\delta = 0.25$): the superiority of architecture-aware over generic prompting, established descriptively in \Cref{sec:results:stage2,sec:results:cross_stage}, is confirmed inferentially. Third, the lone exception is the Stage~2 versus Stage~3 contrast. Although the Wilcoxon test technically flags it ($p = 2.3 \times 10^{-3}$), the difference favours Stage~2 (80 improved versus 103 worsened), the Nemenyi test finds the pair statistically indistinguishable (rank gap $0.16$ below the critical difference of $0.27$), and Cliff's $\delta$ is a negligible $-0.09$. In the portfolio setting, the feedback loop's \emph{aggregate} \gls{ipc} is statistically inseparable from the one-shot architecture-aware stage---precisely consistent with the reading in \Cref{sec:results:stage3} that the loop's contribution is breadth and reliability rather than a higher aggregate ceiling.

The effect sizes deserve a word of calibration. The largest, $\delta = 0.30$ for S0 versus S2, falls in the ``Small'' band of the Cohen-equivalent benchmarks of \citeauthor{Romano2006}~\cite{Romano2006}. In the context of domain-independent planning, however---improving heterogeneous domains across four architecturally disparate planners without altering a single line of planner source code---a consistent small effect is a substantive outcome rather than a weak one. Reassuringly, all five baseline- and generic-referenced confidence intervals exclude zero (\Cref{fig:stat_forest}), so the improvements are robust as well as significant.

\subsection{Factor Effects: Methodology over Model}
\label{sec:results:statistical_validation:factors}

A final set of Kruskal--Wallis tests asks which experimental factors actually drive the \gls{ipc} outcome, stage by stage (\Cref{tab:stat_kw}). The result for the \gls{llm} factor is the most consequential statistical finding of the thesis. Under the generic prompt of Stage~1, the choice of \gls{llm} matters strongly ($H = 25.1$, $p = 1.4 \times 10^{-5}$)---without guidance, some models succeed and others fail. Under the architecture-aware prompt of Stage~2, and again under the feedback loop of Stage~3, the same factor becomes \emph{statistically non-significant} ($p = 0.65$ and $p = 0.18$). The methodology erases the model gap: the large baseline-to-S2/S3 improvements are produced by the prompt engineering and feedback design, not by a more capable model. Practically, this means the approach is portable---it does not depend on access to any particular frontier \gls{llm}.

The other two factors behave as the architectural thesis predicts. The \emph{planner} effect on \gls{ipc} gain is significant at every stage and strengthens monotonically ($H = 26.6 \rightarrow 39.9 \rightarrow 76.7$): rather than homogenising the planners, the pipeline progressively \emph{amplifies} their architectural differences, widening the gap between the responsive heuristic searchers and the rigid DecStar as the methodology deepens. The \emph{domain} effect tells the same story from the prompt side: its $H$ statistic leaps from a marginal $10.6$ under the generic prompt to $85.9$ under the architecture-aware prompt. A generic prompt acts almost uniformly across domains---the model is effectively guessing---whereas architecture-aware guidance lets it detect and exploit domain-specific structure, an eightfold sharpening that is itself strong evidence for the central methodological claim.

Taken together, the statistical suite confirms every substantive element of the descriptive analysis. The stage progression is real ($p \approx 10^{-57}$), architecture-aware prompting decisively outperforms generic prompting ($p = 2.2 \times 10^{-21}$), the gains are robust (all baseline-referenced confidence intervals exclude zero) and contextually meaningful, and the methodology simultaneously equalises models and amplifies architectural and domain structure. The single honest negative---that the feedback loop does not raise the aggregate ceiling beyond Stage~2 in the portfolio setting---is reported as such, its value located instead in the breadth and reliability documented in \Cref{sec:results:stage3}. \Cref{sec:results:prior_work} closes the chapter by situating these results against the prior work that motivated them.

\section{Comparison with Prior Work}
\label{sec:results:prior_work}

The present thesis extends the foundational study of \citeauthor{Elis2025}~\cite{Elis2025}, which first probed whether \glspl{llm} could restructure \gls{pddl} domains through static prompting (zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought variants swept across several models and temperatures). That study established the feasibility of the idea but exposed two ceilings: an overall semantic validity rate near 49\%, and a weak, regression-prone optimisation rate of roughly 14--26\% of configurations improving over the baseline. The architecture-aware prompts and feedback loop developed here were designed precisely to lift those ceilings. \Cref{tab:elis_comparison} sets the two studies side by side.

On both headline metrics the gap is large, but the two gains must be attributed with care. The leap in validity (49\%~$\rightarrow$~93\%) is only partly methodological: a substantial share owes to the generational advance in the underlying models, since the 2026 models used here treat \gls{pddl} syntax as essentially solved (the strongest two reach 100\% syntactic validity), whereas the prior study predates them. The leap in improvement rate ($\sim$20\%~$\rightarrow$~80.9\%), by contrast, cannot be explained by better models alone---architecture-aware prompting and the feedback loop are mechanisms entirely absent from the prior framework, and it is they, not model scale, that convert the inert generic reorderings of Stage~1 into the targeted, regression-free improvements of Stages~2 and~3.

A second caveat is equally important: the cross-thesis comparison is not a controlled experiment. The two studies overlap in only one planner (Madagascar) and one domain (Barman), and differ further in model generation and instance count, so the absolute figures in \Cref{tab:elis_comparison} describe trajectories rather than a like-for-like contest. The rigorous causal evidence for the methodological claim therefore rests not on this external comparison but on the \emph{internal} ablation of this thesis: the Stage~1 versus Stage~2 contrast (\Cref{sec:results:stage2}) holds the planners, domains, and models fixed and varies only the prompt design, and \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation} confirmed that architecture-awareness alone produces a significant improvement ($p = 2.2 \times 10^{-21}$). Read together, the prior work establishes where the field stood, while the controlled ablation establishes why architecture-awareness advances it---realising, with empirical support, the planner-aware configuration that \citeauthor{Elis2025} identified as a direction for future work. The boundaries of that claim, including the limited planner and domain coverage just noted, are examined systematically in \Cref{ch:threats}.
