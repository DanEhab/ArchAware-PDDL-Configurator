\chapter{Threats to Validity}\label{ch:threats}
% Dedicated chapter discussing potential threats following the standard four-type framework

As with any empirical study, the findings reported in \Cref{ch:results} are subject to threats to validity, discussed here under the standard four-type classification~\cite{Wohlin2012}: internal validity (causal integrity), external validity (generalisability), construct validity (measurement appropriateness), and statistical conclusion validity (inferential reliability).
For each threat the discussion identifies a concrete mitigation already built into the experimental design or, where no mitigation exists, states the residual risk explicitly.

\section{Internal Validity}
\label{sec:threats:internal}

Internal validity concerns whether the observed performance differences can be causally attributed to the experimental treatment---the prompt strategy and feedback loop---rather than to confounding factors.

\paragraph{LLM non-determinism.}
Although all models are queried with a temperature of~$0.0$ (\Cref{sec:setup:hyperparameters}), provider-side implementation details such as request batching, floating-point non-determinism across hardware, and undocumented internal reasoning exploration (particularly for deep-reasoning models like DeepSeek-R1) may cause the same prompt to produce slightly different outputs on repeated calls.
Because each configuration was generated exactly once rather than averaged over multiple runs, a single stochastic deviation could shift a triple from ``improved'' to ``not improved'' or vice versa; since the selected planners are otherwise deterministic, this generation step is the pipeline's sole stochastic component, and repeating the full generation--validation--execution cycle to quantify its variance was infeasible within the computational budget of a bachelor thesis.
We mitigate this by reporting population-level statistics over 68 contestable triples rather than drawing conclusions from individual cases, and by confirming the stage effect with the Friedman omnibus test ($p \approx 10^{-57}$, \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation:stage}).

\paragraph{Hardware-induced timing noise.}
All planner executions ran on a single dedicated machine (\Cref{sec:setup:hardware}) with Docker containers constrained to one CPU core and 8~GB of memory, eliminating cross-machine variance.
However, background operating-system processes and Docker's own overhead introduce residual wall-clock noise.
Because the \gls{ipc} score is derived directly from wall-clock runtime, small timing fluctuations could affect per-instance scores.
Furthermore, the 360-second timeout boundary creates an artificial ceiling for the \gls{ipc} score, which inherently bounds the observable variance for slower planners.
We control for this measurement constraint by using the paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test, which compares each instance's configured runtime against its own baseline counterpart, thereby cancelling any instance-level difficulty bias.

\paragraph{Order effects in planner execution.}
Problem instances were executed sequentially within each planner container.
If a planner maintains internal caches or exhibits warm-up effects, earlier instances could systematically differ from later ones.
This risk is mitigated by the deterministic, stateless nature of all four selected planners: each instance is loaded, solved, and discarded independently, with no state carried between runs.

\paragraph{Feedback-loop information leakage.}
In Stage~3, the \gls{llm} receives execution telemetry from the target planner as part of the critique prompt (\Cref{sec:design:stage3}).
Because the telemetry reveals which instances timed out, the model could hypothetically ``overfit'' its reordering to the specific set of 15 selected instances rather than improving the domain in general.
However, the initial instance selection was rigorously randomised using a fixed global seed (\texttt{seed=42}) prior to execution (\Cref{sec:setup:hyperparameters}), ensuring the sample itself is unbiased.
Furthermore, the model receives only aggregate per-instance solve/timeout verdicts and runtime, not the internal search trace, limiting its capacity for instance-level exploitation.

\section{External Validity}
\label{sec:threats:external}

External validity concerns whether the findings generalise beyond the specific experimental conditions.

\paragraph{Domain coverage.}
The study evaluates five \gls{ipc} benchmark domains selected for structural diversity (\Cref{sec:setup:domains}).
Although the domains span the full range of every quantified structural metric ($\lvert A \rvert \in [1,12]$, $\lvert O_t \rvert \in [0,7]$, max arity $\in [2,6]$), they represent only the STRIPS subset of \gls{pddl} and exclude temporal, numeric-fluent-heavy, and ADL domains.
Results may not transfer to these richer language fragments.

\paragraph{Planner coverage.}
Four planners were selected to maximise architectural diversity (\Cref{sec:setup:planners}), but the planning community encompasses many additional paradigms---HTN planners, temporal planners, optimal-track planners, and portfolio systems---that are not represented.
The observed architectural responsiveness ranking (\gls{bfws}~$>$~LAMA~$>$~Madagascar~$>$~DecStar) is specific to the four tested architectures and should not be extrapolated to untested paradigms without further experimentation.

\paragraph{LLM model versions.}
The four \glspl{llm} used (\Cref{sec:setup:llms}) represent a snapshot of the frontier as of early~2026.
Model capabilities evolve rapidly; newer or fine-tuned models may produce qualitatively different reorderings.
API version pinning (\Cref{sec:setup:hyperparameters}) ensures reproducibility of the reported results but cannot guarantee that the same conclusions hold for future model generations.
The finding that architecture-aware prompting renders the \gls{llm} choice statistically non-significant (\Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation:factors}) partially mitigates this concern, as it suggests the methodology is more important than the specific model.

\section{Construct Validity}
\label{sec:threats:construct}

Construct validity concerns whether the chosen measurements faithfully capture the intended theoretical constructs.

\paragraph{IPC score as a proxy for planner efficiency.}
The primary outcome measure, the \gls{ipc} score (\Cref{eq:ipc_score}), rewards fast solutions and penalises unsolved instances but does not capture plan quality (e.g., plan length or action cost) as a first-class dimension.
A configuration that accelerates search but produces longer plans would register as an improvement under this metric.
We partially address this by reporting plan cost alongside the \gls{ipc} score in the baseline characterisation (\Cref{sec:results:stage0:cost}), but the statistical tests are conducted on \gls{ipc} scores only.

\paragraph{Operationalisation of ``architecture-awareness.''}
The architecture-aware prompts (\Cref{sec:setup:arch_aware_prompt}) were manually crafted by analysing each planner's source code and deriving reordering rules traceable to specific internal components.
This operationalisation is one of many possible encodings of architectural knowledge; a different analyst might emphasise different components or formulate rules differently.
The design principles of traceability, differentiation, and actionability (\Cref{sec:setup:arch_aware_prompt}) constrain but do not eliminate this subjectivity.

\paragraph{Semantic equivalence verification.}
The fourth validation level (V4) relies on structural comparison of parsed \gls{pddl} trees---checking that the sets of predicates, actions, preconditions, and effects are identical modulo ordering---rather than on formal model-theoretic verification.
While this structural check is conservative (it rejects any addition, deletion, or renaming), it cannot detect subtle semantic changes that preserve syntactic structure, such as a reordering that exploits an implicit assumption in a planner's grounding algorithm.
In practice, the zero semantic-error rate among validated domains (\Cref{sec:results:cross_stage:validation}) suggests the risk is low, but it is not formally excluded.

\paragraph{Improvement-detection threshold.}
The three-condition improvement gate (\Cref{sec:design:stage2:improvement}) uses a relaxed significance threshold of $\alpha = 0.25$ for the within-pipeline Wilcoxon test, deliberately trading Type~I error risk for sensitivity in a screening context.
A stricter threshold would classify fewer configurations as improved; a more lenient one would admit more noise.
The confirmatory analysis of \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation} re-tests all stage comparisons at the conventional $\alpha = 0.05$ (Bonferroni-corrected to $0.0083$), confirming that the headline findings survive the stricter standard.

\section{Statistical Conclusion Validity}
\label{sec:threats:statistical}

Statistical conclusion validity concerns the reliability of the statistical inferences drawn from the data.

\paragraph{Sample size per experimental cell.}
Each (planner, domain, \gls{llm}) triple comprises 15 paired observations.
While sufficient for the Wilcoxon signed-rank test (which requires as few as six non-zero differences for adequate power), this sample size limits the detection of very small effects.
The population-level tests (\Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation}), which pool all 300 observations, achieve substantially higher power.

\paragraph{Multiple comparisons.}
The six pairwise stage comparisons introduce a family-wise Type~I error risk.
We control this with Bonferroni correction ($\alpha = 0.05 / 6 = 0.0083$), the most conservative standard adjustment.
All five significant pairwise results survive this correction comfortably ($p \leq 2.7 \times 10^{-7}$, \Cref{tab:stat_pairwise}), so the risk of a false positive among the reported significant comparisons is negligible.

\paragraph{Effect-size interpretation.}
Cliff's~$\delta$ effect sizes are interpreted using the conventional thresholds of \citeauthor{Romano2006}~\cite{Romano2006} ($|\delta| < 0.147$: negligible; $< 0.33$: small; $< 0.474$: medium; $\geq 0.474$: large).
These thresholds are domain-agnostic and may overstate or understate the practical significance of improvements in the specific context of AI planning, where even small runtime reductions can affect competition rankings.
As noted in \Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation:stage}, a consistent small effect ($\delta = 0.30$) across four architecturally diverse planners and five heterogeneous domains---achieved without modifying any planner code---constitutes a substantive result in this field.

\paragraph{Non-parametric test assumptions.}
The Wilcoxon signed-rank test assumes that the paired differences are symmetric around their median, and the Friedman test assumes that the observations are ordinal and that the blocks (instances) are independent.
The first assumption is difficult to verify with the heavy-tailed \gls{ipc}-score distributions observed (\Cref{sec:results:statistical_validation:selection}); any violation would reduce test power rather than inflate it, making the reported significant results conservative.
Instance independence holds by construction, as each planner run is an isolated Docker execution with no shared state.
